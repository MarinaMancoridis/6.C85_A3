Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • Insight 1. The number of new COVID-19 cases increases in the winter months, as demonstrated by the increased thickness of the red lines on the top of the spiral graph.
  • Insight 2. The number of new COVID-19 cases has generally increased from 2020 (when there were around no cases). Since the beginning of the pandemic, the number of new cases reached its lowest point in July of 2021 and its highest point in January of 2022.
  • Insight 3. The number of new COVID-19 cases spiked in July of 2021, as indicated by the fanning out of the red spiral in the topmost portion of the graph. The trend suggest that the number of new cases might increase further after that point.
  • Insight 4. The number of new COVID-19 cases decreases in the summer months, as demonstrated by the decreased thickness of the red lines on the top of the spiral graph.
  • Insight 5. The year 2020 had much fewer COVID-19 cases than either 2021 or 2022. The number of cases in the winter months of 2021 was generally more than the number of cases in the winter months of 2022.

Design Critique:

  • Critique 1 (prominance of information). This visualization tells a story—a sweeping narrative of a pandemic in motion. It captures the broad, unmistakable trends: COVID-19 cases climbing from January 2020 to January 2022 and surging notably during the winter months. In doing so, it provides a clear, macro-level picture of seasonal peaks and gradual increases. Yet, this focus on the overarching pattern comes at a price. The design sacrifices the ability to easily compare exact numbers between different time periods. Rather than offering a straightforward way to quantify month-to-month differences, it forces the viewer to mentally juggle the lengths of curved red bands and decode redundant labels. Even the legend, capped at 150K, falls short when some spikes go beyond that threshold, muddying our sense of scale. Moreover, the visualization displays redundant information by showing the number of cases both above and below the black line. The tradeoff that the designers made between narrative and ease of quantatative analysis is clear: the visualization was made to enable the reader to easily see the general trends of the number of new cases over time and to provide a general assessment of seasonal differences in cases.
  • Critique 2 (intended audience). The visualization in the New York Times is designed for a broad audience, at a moment when public anxiety about COVID-19 was at its peak. Its role at the start of the article is simple: provide a clear, digestible snapshot of the pandemic's trends—rising cases, especially in winter. But this clarity comes at a cost. Context is sacrificed in exchange for narrative flow. While the text of the article discusses that the number of cases is often delayed during the two weeks beginning shortly before Christmas until shortly after New Year’s Day and that SARS-CoV-2 and its variants are likely to play a role in the progression of the disease, the visualization itself lacks any context. Contextual annotations could have been very helpful for anxiety-ridden readers; if the designers had included information about key policy shifts, social distancing practices, or reporting delays, readers could have better understood the reasons behind the trends. The lack of contextual annotations, of course, is the results of an intentional choice made by the designers in the tradeoff between providing a clear narrative and providing context for the data. Their ultimate choice invites readers to dive into the article for more details, but fundamentally limits their capacity to draw data-driven insights at the beginning of the article.
  • Critique 3 (visual encodings). The visualization uses a spiral graph to represent the number of new COVID-19 cases in the United States. This graph is a unique and creative way to represent the reader; as a result, it immediately draws intrigue. However, it is unclear that the reader is supposed to read the graph from the center outwards. This sacrifices the overarching temporal narrative of the data. Moreover, the scale of the graph is difficult to interpret: later years extend over a larger area than earlier ones, leaving it unclear if the red segments are proportionally adjusted. These design choices sacrifice the narrative structure of the data and introduces ambiguity in how to interpret scale measurements. However, the spiral introduces a refreshing visual twist in an era of information overload and heightened anxiety, trading off the clarity of a coherent narrative for aesthetic intrigue.

Visualization Sketches

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • Rationale 1. My motivation behind generating this sketch was to provide a visualization that includes context for the data. This was accomplished with gray annotations that denoted key case reporting information, vaccine releases, and policy changes. This is in response to the critique that the original visualization lacked context for the presented data.
  • Rationale 2. I was hoping that this visualization would communicate the narrative elements of the data by situating the information in broader, clarifying context. This is important because the original visualization was published during a time of high public anxiety.
  • Rationale 3. Some things worked well in this visualization. For example, the split y-axis highlights a trend that was relatively hidden in the first visualization: the last month in the dataset had the highest numer of new cases of COVID-19. This decision was made in response to my critique that the original visualization did not provide a way for readers to make quantitative analyses. Another thing that worked well was using colors to represent different years (with red representing 2022, the year with the highest number of new cases in any month). Finally, there is a high data-to-ink ratio. White space was limited without the visualization feeling cramped.
  • Rationale 4. Some things did not work well in this visualization. For example, it is misleading that only January and July are the labeled months. While this decision was intended to reduce clutter, it unnecessarily highlights these months. Moreover, the annotations imply causal reasons for the trends in the data, which may have been influenced by a variety of factors. This could be misleading to the readers. Finally, the split y-axis may be confusing to readers who are not familiar with this type of visualization.
  • Rationale 5. Future work on this visualization could include more annotations to make it obvious that the annotations are not causal. Rather, they are included to situate the data in a broader historical narrative of the time period. Moreover, the color palette could be adjusted to be more accessible to colorblind readers. Finally, the number of existing cases could be included in a side-by-side graphic to provide context with implications for hospital capacity, public health policy, and societal morale.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • Rationale 1. My motivation behind generating this sketch was to provide a visualization that feels more human and personal. This was accomplished by using photographs to augment the data. The data journalism form factor of this illustration is intended to make the data more relatable, visceral, and emotional to the reader. The captions are intended to provide context for the data. This structure evokes a historical narrative of the COVID-19 pandemic, bringing the data "to life".
